1.  Explain the relationship between internal energy change and heat transfer in a system undergoing an isothermal process. Support your explanation with a solved example relevant to the definition of calorie.
Sample Answer:
Okay, here's a sample answer appropriate for a CBSE Class 11-12 student, drawing on the (admittedly limited) NCERT content provided. I'll need to make some logical jumps and assumptions based on standard thermodynamics concepts:

**Answer:**

In thermodynamics, the internal energy (U) of a system represents the total energy contained within it. This includes kinetic energy of molecules and potential energy from intermolecular forces. The First Law of Thermodynamics states that the change in internal energy (ΔU) of a system is equal to the heat (q) added to the system minus the work (w) done by the system:

**ΔU = q - w**

Now, let's consider an **isothermal process**. The defining characteristic of an isothermal process is that the temperature of the system remains *constant* throughout the process.  Crucially, for an ideal gas in an isothermal process, the internal energy (U) depends *only* on temperature.  Therefore, if the temperature (T) is constant, the change in internal energy (ΔU) is **zero**:

**ΔU = 0** (for an isothermal process with an ideal gas)

Substituting this into the First Law of Thermodynamics, we get:

**0 = q - w**

Rearranging this equation, we find:

**q = w**

This equation reveals the relationship between internal energy change and heat transfer in an isothermal process: **In an isothermal process, the heat absorbed by the system is entirely converted into work done by the system, and vice versa.** There is no change in the internal energy of the system.  All the energy transferred as heat is used to perform work.

**Solved Example (related to the definition of a calorie):**

Let's consider the isothermal expansion of an ideal gas. While the provided content doesn't give a specific example, we can use the concept of the calorie to illustrate the energy transfer involved. Imagine a system containing 1 mole of an ideal gas at a constant temperature of 25°C (298 K). The gas expands isothermally, performing work on its surroundings.

Suppose the work done by the gas during this expansion is 4184 Joules. Knowing the definition of a calorie as the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of water by 1 degree Celsius (approximately 4.184 J), we can relate this work to the heat absorbed.

Since the process is isothermal, ΔU = 0. Therefore, q = w.

This means that the gas absorbed 4184 J of heat from its surroundings during the expansion.  We can also express this as:

Heat absorbed (q) = 4184 J / 4.184 J/calorie = 1000 calories = 1 kilocalorie (kcal)

**Therefore, in this isothermal expansion, the gas absorbed 1 kilocalorie of heat from the surroundings, and this entire amount of heat was converted into the work of expansion.**  This illustrates how, in an isothermal process, heat transfer and work are directly related, and the change in internal energy is zero.

**Explanation of Relevance to Calorie Definition:**

The example connects the theoretical concept of isothermal work to the practical unit of energy – the calorie. Although the example itself isn't *directly* about raising the temperature of water, it helps students visualize that energy transfer (in this case, heat absorbed) can be expressed in calories. It also reinforces the idea that energy can be converted from one form (heat) to another (work) in a measurable way.

**Important Considerations for Students:**

*   This explanation assumes an *ideal gas* undergoing an isothermal process. Real gases may have more complex behaviors.
*   The concept of *reversible* isothermal processes is implied. In reality, processes may be irreversible, leading to slightly different results.
*   The example is simplified. Actual calculations of isothermal work involve integration of the pressure-volume relationship.

2.  Consider a hypothetical process where a system's internal energy increases. Discuss two distinct scenarios, one involving heat and the other not, that could lead to this increase. Explain how the "calorie" concept applies to each scenario.
Sample Answer:
Okay, here's a sample answer suitable for a CBSE Class 11-12 student, based on the (limited) provided NCERT chapter content, addressing the assignment question.  I'll focus on what we *can* infer from the brief summary and the mention of "calorie" and isothermal processes:

**Answer:**

A system's internal energy (U) represents the total energy stored within it, including kinetic and potential energy of its constituent particles. An increase in internal energy (ΔU > 0) means the system's energy content has gone up. Let's explore two scenarios where this can happen, one involving heat and one without.

**Scenario 1: Heating a System (Heat Involved)**

Imagine a metal block placed in a furnace. The furnace provides *heat* to the metal block.  In this scenario, the **calorie** concept comes directly into play.  A calorie (or kilocalorie, which is more commonly used) is a unit of energy.  Specifically, it's the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of water by 1 degree Celsius.

As the metal block absorbs heat from the furnace, the kinetic energy of its atoms increases. These atoms vibrate more vigorously. This increased atomic motion translates directly into an increase in the system's internal energy (ΔU > 0). The amount of heat *absorbed* by the metal block can be quantified in calories (or kilocalories). This heat input is directly responsible for raising the block's internal energy and, consequently, its temperature (unless a phase change occurs).

**Scenario 2: Work Done *on* the System (No Direct Heat Transfer)**

Consider a gas contained within a cylinder fitted with a piston. Now, imagine *compressing* the gas rapidly by pushing the piston inward.  If this compression is done quickly, we can assume the process is essentially *adiabatic* (meaning no heat is exchanged with the surroundings).

In this case, the *work* done *on* the gas by the piston is converted directly into internal energy. As the gas is compressed, the gas molecules collide more frequently and with greater force, increasing their kinetic energy. This increased kinetic energy of the gas molecules increases the internal energy (ΔU > 0).

In this scenario, the **calorie** concept is relevant indirectly. While no *heat* is transferred, the *work* done is equivalent to a certain amount of energy that *could* have been supplied as heat (and hence, measured in calories).  The First Law of Thermodynamics dictates that the change in internal energy equals the heat added to the system minus the work done *by* the system. If no heat is added (adiabatic), the change in internal energy is *equal to the negative of the work done by the system*.  Since we are doing work *on* the system, the work term is negative, making ΔU positive.  The equivalent amount of energy could theoretically be expressed in calories.

**In Summary:**

Both scenarios lead to an increase in the system's internal energy. In the first, heat is directly added and measured in calories. In the second, work is done on the system, and while no heat is transferred directly, the equivalent energy increase can still be conceptually linked to the calorie as a unit of energy.

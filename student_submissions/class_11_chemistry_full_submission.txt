**1. Question 1: Explain the significance of the limiting reactant in a chemical reaction. How does identifying the limiting reactant help in determining the theoretical yield of the product?**

**Answer:** The limiting reactant is the reactant that is completely consumed first in a chemical reaction.  It determines the maximum amount of product that can be formed.  Identifying it allows accurate calculation of the theoretical yield because the reaction stops when the limiting reactant is used up, regardless of the amount of other reactants present.

**2. Question 2: A compound is found to contain 60% carbon, 5% hydrogen, and 35% nitrogen by mass. Determine the empirical formula of the compound. Show your work.**

**Answer:**

*   C: 60g / 12 g/mol = 5 mol
*   H: 5g / 1 g/mol = 5 mol

**3. Question 3: Define the terms 'empirical formula' and 'molecular formula'. How is the molecular formula related to the empirical formula, and what additional information is needed to determine the molecular formula?**

**Answer:** The empirical formula is the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound. The molecular formula is the actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule.  The molecular formula is a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.  To determine the molecular formula, you need the molar mass of the compound.

**4. Question 4: What is the significance of the equilibrium constant (Kc) for a reversible reaction? How does the value of Kc indicate the relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium?**

**Answer:** Kc indicates the extent to which a reaction proceeds to completion at equilibrium. A large Kc value (>1) means products are favored, and the reaction goes mostly to completion. A small Kc value (<1) means reactants are favored, and the reaction hardly proceeds. A Kc close to 1 means roughly equal amounts of reactants and products exist at equilibrium.

**5. Question 5: Explain what pH measures. How are the pH and hydrogen ion concentration [H+] related? What does a low pH value indicate about a solution?**

**Answer:** pH measures the acidity or basicity of a solution. pH is the negative logarithm of the hydrogen ion concentration: pH = -log[H+]. A low pH value indicates a high [H+] and therefore an acidic solution.

**6. Question 6: A student performs a calculation and obtains the result 2.34567. If the input data has only three significant figures, how should the student report the final answer and why?**

**8. Question 8: Write the mathematical expression for Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle and explain its significance in determining the position and momentum of a subatomic particle.**

**Answer:** The Heisenberg uncertainty principle is: Δx * Δp ≥ h/4π, where Δx is the uncertainty in position, Δp is the uncertainty in momentum, and h is Planck's constant. It states that it is impossible to know both the position and momentum of a subatomic particle with perfect accuracy simultaneously. The more accurately one is known, the less accurately the other is known.

**9. Question 9: Define "quantum number" and explain the significance of each of the four quantum numbers (n, l, ml, and ms) in describing the properties of an electron in an atom.**

**Answer:** A quantum number is a number that describes a property of an electron in an atom.

*   **n (Principal Quantum Number):** Energy level (shell).  Higher n = higher energy and larger orbital size.
*   **l (Azimuthal/Angular Momentum Quantum Number):** Shape of the orbital (subshell).  l = 0 (s), l = 1 (p), l = 2 (d), l = 3 (f).
*   **ml (Magnetic Quantum Number):**  Orientation of the orbital in space.  Values range from -l to +l, including 0.
*   **ms (Spin Quantum Number):**  Spin of the electron (+1/2 or -1/2).

**4. Acid-Base Equilibria and pH Calculation:** A solution of acetic acid (CH<sub>3</sub>COOH) has a concentration of 0.1 M. If the acid dissociation constant (Ka) for acetic acid is 1.8 x 10<sup>-5</sup>, calculate the pH of the solution. Explain the relationship between Ka and pH.

**Answer:**

CH<sub>3</sub>COOH(aq) ⇌ H<sup>+</sup>(aq) + CH<sub>3</sub>COO<sup>-</sup>(aq)

1.8 x 10<sup>-5</sup> = x<sup>2</sup> / 0.1

x<sup>2</sup> = 1.8 x 10<sup>-6</sup>

x = √(1.8 x 10<sup>-6</sup>) = 1.34 x 10<sup>-3</sup> M = [H+]

pH = -log[H+] = -log(1.34 x 10<sup>-3</sup>) = 2.87

*Relationship between Ka and pH:* A smaller Ka indicates a weaker acid and a higher pH (less acidic). Ka is directly related to the [H+] concentration, which inversely affects the pH.

If we have 4 moles of H<sub>2</sub> and only 1 mole of O<sub>2</sub>, O<sub>2</sub> is the limiting reactant because 2 moles of H<sub>2</sub> are required for every 1 mole of O<sub>2</sub>. **2. Question 2 (10 Marks):**

**(a)** Explain the significance of the equilibrium constant, K<sub>c</sub>, in predicting the extent of a reversible reaction. How does the value of K<sub>c</sub> influence the relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium? Illustrate your answer with examples. (4 Marks)

**(b)** Consider the following reversible reaction:

N<sub>2</sub>(g) + 3H<sub>2</sub>(g) ⇌ 2NH<sub>3</sub>(g)

At a certain temperature, the equilibrium concentrations are found to be [N<sub>2</sub>] = 2.0 M, [H<sub>2</sub>] = 3.0 M, and [NH<sub>3</sub>] = 0.5 M. Calculate the equilibrium constant, K<sub>c</sub>, for this reaction. If the volume of the container is doubled, predict the shift in equilibrium (towards reactants or products) using Le Chatelier's principle and explain the effect on K<sub>c</sub>. (6 Marks)

**Answer:**

**(a)**

The equilibrium constant, K<sub>c</sub>, is a quantitative measure of the extent to which a reversible reaction proceeds to completion at a given temperature. It represents the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, each raised to the power of their stoichiometric coefficients.

The value of K<sub>c</sub> directly indicates the relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium:

*   **Large K<sub>c</sub> (K<sub>c</sub> >> 1):** The equilibrium lies to the right, favoring the formation of products.  At equilibrium, there will be a much higher concentration of products than reactants. *Example:*  If K<sub>c</sub> = 1000 for a reaction A ⇌ B, then at equilibrium, [B] will be significantly greater than [A].
*   **Small K<sub>c</sub> (K<sub>c</sub> << 1):** The equilibrium lies to the left, favoring the reactants. At equilibrium, there will be a much higher concentration of reactants than products. *Example:* If K<sub>c</sub> = 0.001 for a reaction A ⇌ B, then at equilibrium, [A] will be significantly greater than [B].
*   **K<sub>c</sub> ≈ 1:**  The concentrations of reactants and products at equilibrium are roughly comparable.

**(b)**

*K<sub>c</sub> Calculation:*

K<sub>c</sub> = [NH<sub>3</sub>]<sup>2</sup> / ([N<sub>2</sub>] * [H<sub>2</sub>]<sup>3</sup>)

K<sub>c</sub> = (0.5)<sup>2</sup> / (2.0 * (3.0)<sup>3</sup>) = 0.25 / (2.0 * 27) = 0.25 / 54 = 0.00463 (or 4.63 x 10<sup>-3</sup>)

*Effect of Volume Change:*

Doubling the volume will *decrease* the pressure. According to Le Chatelier's principle, the equilibrium will shift to relieve the stress. In this case, the stress is decreased pressure, and the equilibrium will shift towards the side with *more* moles of gas. The reactants side has 1 mole of N<sub>2</sub> + 3 moles of H<sub>2</sub> = 4 moles of gas, while the product side has 2 moles of NH<sub>3</sub> gas. Therefore, the equilibrium will shift to the *left* (towards the reactants).

*Effect on K<sub>c</sub>:* K<sub>c</sub> is a function of temperature. Changing the volume (or pressure) at the *same temperature* will *not* change the value of K<sub>c</sub>. The reaction will shift to re-establish equilibrium, but K<sub>c</sub> will remain constant at that temperature.

    *   *ms (Spin Quantum Number):* Describes the intrinsic angular momentum of the electron, which is quantized and referred to as spin. It can have two values: +1/2 (spin up) or -1/2 (spin down).

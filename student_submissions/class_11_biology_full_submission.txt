1.  **Question 1: Define biological species according to Mayr's concept. Explain why this definition might be challenging to apply to extinct organisms.**

    **Answer:** According to Mayr, a biological species is a group of populations whose members can interbreed in nature and produce viable, fertile offspring, but are reproductively isolated from other such groups. This definition is challenging to apply to extinct organisms because we cannot directly observe or test their ability to interbreed. We lack direct evidence of reproductive compatibility.

2.  **Question 2: Using the example of *Mangifera indica*, explain the importance of binomial nomenclature in biology. Why is it important to have a standardized naming system?**

    **Answer:** *Mangifera indica* is the scientific name for mango. Binomial nomenclature is important because it provides a universally recognized and standardized way to identify each species. It is important to have a standardized naming system because common names can vary by region and language, causing confusion. A standardized system ensures that scientists worldwide are referring to the same organism.

3.  **Question 3: Insects are a very diverse group. List *four* key characteristics that define the Insecta class.**

    **Answer:** Four key characteristics of insects are:
    *   Three-part body (head, thorax, abdomen)
    *   Three pairs of legs (six legs)
    *   Usually possess wings (one or two pairs)
    *   Possess antennae

4.  **Question 4: Explain the significance of the statement "cells arise from pre-existing cells" in the context of cell theory.**

    **Answer:**  The statement "cells arise from pre-existing cells" (from the cell theory) signifies that all cells originate from the division of previously existing cells. This refutes the idea of spontaneous generation and emphasizes the continuity of life.

5.  **Question 5: Briefly describe the key events that occur during Interphase of the cell cycle and explain its importance.**

    **Answer:** During interphase, the cell grows, performs its normal functions, and duplicates its DNA. It consists of G1, S, and G2 phases. The S phase is where DNA replicates. Interphase is crucial because it prepares the cell for division by ensuring that it has enough resources and a complete set of genetic material for each daughter cell.

6.  **Question 6: Differentiate between karyokinesis and cytokinesis. Why is it important for these processes to occur correctly?**

    **Answer:** Karyokinesis is the division of the nucleus, while cytokinesis is the division of the cytoplasm. Karyokinesis ensures equal distribution of chromosomes into daughter nuclei. Cytokinesis physically separates the two daughter cells. Correct execution is vital to ensure each daughter cell receives a complete set of chromosomes and cellular components, preventing genetic abnormalities or non-viable cells.

7.  **Question 7: Compare and contrast mitosis and meiosis with respect to their purpose and the genetic makeup of the resulting daughter cells.**

    **Answer:** Mitosis is for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction, producing two identical diploid daughter cells. Meiosis is for sexual reproduction, producing four genetically diverse haploid daughter cells (gametes). Mitosis maintains the chromosome number, while meiosis halves it.

8.  **Question 8: A cell in G1 phase has 16 chromosomes. How many chromatids will it have in the G2 phase? Explain your reasoning.**

    **Answer:** In the G1 phase, the cell has 16 chromosomes, each with one chromatid (single DNA molecule). During the S phase, each chromosome replicates, resulting in two identical sister chromatids attached at the centromere. Therefore, in the G2 phase, the cell will have 16 chromosomes, each with 2 chromatids, for a total of 32 chromatids.

9.  **Question 9: What is the significance of Meiosis I? Explain how it contributes to genetic diversity.**

    **Answer:** Meiosis I is significant because it separates homologous chromosomes, reducing the chromosome number from diploid to haploid. It contributes to genetic diversity through crossing over (exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes) and independent assortment of chromosomes.

10. **Question 10: What is the G0 phase of the cell cycle, and what are the possible fates of a cell that enters this phase?**

    **Answer:** The G0 phase is a resting phase where cells exit the cell cycle and stop dividing. Cells in G0 are still metabolically active. A cell in G0 can either remain in this state indefinitely (quiescence) or re-enter the cell cycle if stimulated.

This reproductive isolation, despite physical proximity, defines them as distinct species. Mayr's concept highlights the importance of gene flow and reproductive compatibility in maintaining species boundaries.

**Answer:** The cell cycle is fundamental to multicellular organisms because it governs growth, repair, and reproduction. The cell cycle ensures that new cells are produced in a controlled and coordinated manner, maintaining tissue homeostasis.

    *   Cell growth and increase in size.

    *   The entire genome is duplicated accurately.
    *   Each chromosome is copied to produce two identical sister chromatids.
    *   **Importance:** S phase ensures that each daughter cell receives a complete and identical copy of the genetic information. Errors in DNA replication can lead to mutations and cell dysfunction.

    *   Further cell growth and protein synthesis.
    *   Duplication of organelles like centrioles (in animal cells).
    *   Checking for DNA damage and ensuring DNA replication is complete.
    *   **Importance:** G2 ensures the cell has all the necessary components for mitosis and that the DNA is error-free. If damage is detected, the cell cycle can be arrested to allow for repair or programmed cell death (apoptosis).

**6. Question 6: Karyokinesis and Cytokinesis are two distinct processes in cell division. Explain each process in detail and discuss the consequences if Cytokinesis does not occur after Karyokinesis.**

**Answer:** *Karyokinesis* is the process of nuclear division. It occurs in distinct stages: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase. During prophase, the chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, the nuclear envelope breaks down, and the spindle fibers form. In metaphase, the chromosomes align at the metaphase plate. During anaphase, the sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles. In telophase, the chromosomes arrive at the poles, the nuclear envelope reforms, and the chromosomes decondense. The result is two nuclei with identical genetic material.

*Cytokinesis* is the division of the cytoplasm, which usually follows karyokinesis. In animal cells, cytokinesis occurs through the formation of a cleavage furrow that pinches the cell in two. In plant cells, a cell plate forms between the two nuclei, eventually developing into a new cell wall. Cytokinesis results in two separate daughter cells, each with its own nucleus and cytoplasm.

If cytokinesis does not occur after karyokinesis, the result is a multinucleated cell. This cell would contain multiple nuclei within a single cytoplasm. Such cells may have abnormal function and can contribute to uncontrolled cell growth (cancer).

**Answer:**

*   **Prophase:** The 36 chromosomes condense and become visible as duplicated structures, each consisting of two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere. The nuclear envelope breaks down.
*   **Metaphase:** The 36 duplicated chromosomes align at the metaphase plate (equator of the cell). Each chromosome consists of two sister chromatids held together at the centromere.
*   **Anaphase:** The centromeres divide, and the sister chromatids separate, becoming individual chromosomes. There are now 72 chromosomes (36 moving to each pole) migrating towards opposite poles of the cell.
*   **Telophase:** The 36 chromosomes arrive at each pole of the cell and begin to decondense. The nuclear envelope reforms around each set of chromosomes, forming two separate nuclei. Each nucleus contains 36 chromosomes.

**8. Question 8: Explain the significance of the G0 phase in the cell cycle. Under what conditions might a cell enter G0, and what are the possible fates of a cell in this phase? Give examples of cell types that commonly exist in the G0 phase.**

**Answer:** The G0 phase is a state of quiescence or cell cycle arrest. Its significance lies in allowing cells to perform specialized functions without dividing, conserving energy, and preventing uncontrolled cell proliferation.

Conditions that might trigger entry into G0:

*   **Lack of growth factors:** Insufficient external signals promoting cell division.
*   **Nutrient deprivation:** Insufficient resources to support cell division.
*   **Differentiation:** Cell specialization where the cell is programmed to perform a specific function rather than divide.
*   **DNA damage:** If DNA damage is detected, the cell cycle may be halted, and the cell may enter G0.
*   **Cell crowding/contact inhibition:** High cell density can inhibit cell division and induce G0.

Possible fates of a cell in G0:

*   **Quiescence:** The cell remains in a non-dividing state, performing its specialized function. The cell can re-enter the cell cycle if conditions become favorable.
*   **Permanent Arrest:** The cell permanently exits the cell cycle and no longer divides. This can occur due to terminal differentiation or irreversible damage.
*   **Apoptosis:** If the cell is damaged or no longer needed, it may undergo programmed cell death.

Examples of cell types commonly found in G0:

*   **Neurons:** Most mature nerve cells are permanently in G0 and do not divide.
*   **Muscle cells:** Mature muscle cells are often in G0.
*   **Liver cells (hepatocytes):** Liver cells can enter G0 but can also re-enter the cell cycle to repair damage.

**9. Question 9: In Meiosis, crossing over during Prophase I is a significant event. Explain the process of crossing over and discuss its importance in generating genetic variation. How does this contribute to the diversity observed in sexually reproducing organisms?**

**Answer:** Crossing over is a process that occurs during Prophase I of meiosis. Homologous chromosomes pair up to form tetrads (or bivalents). At specific points called chiasmata, non-sister chromatids of the homologous chromosomes exchange segments of DNA. This exchange involves the breaking and rejoining of DNA strands.

Importance in generating genetic variation:

*   **New Combinations of Alleles:** Crossing over shuffles the alleles (different versions of a gene) on each chromosome, creating new combinations that were not present in the parent chromosomes.
*   **Increased Genetic Diversity:** This process significantly increases the genetic diversity of gametes. Each gamete receives a unique combination of genetic information from both parents.

Contribution to diversity in sexually reproducing organisms:

The genetic variation generated by crossing over, combined with independent assortment of chromosomes, is the foundation for the diversity observed in sexually reproducing organisms. This diversity allows populations to adapt to changing environments, resist diseases, and evolve over time. Without crossing over, genetic variation would be greatly reduced, limiting the adaptive potential of populations.

**10. Question 10: A diploid cell undergoes meiosis. If non-disjunction of chromosome 21 occurs during Meiosis II, what will be the chromosome composition of the resulting gametes? Explain the potential consequences if one of these gametes fertilizes a normal gamete.**

**Answer:** Non-disjunction is the failure of chromosomes or sister chromatids to separate properly during cell division. If non-disjunction of chromosome 21 occurs during Meiosis II, it means that the sister chromatids of chromosome 21 fail to separate in one of the daughter cells.

Chromosome composition of the resulting gametes:

*   **Two gametes will be normal:** They will have one copy of chromosome 21 (the usual haploid number).
*   **One gamete will have an extra copy of chromosome 21:** It will have two copies of chromosome 21.
*   **One gamete will be missing chromosome 21:** It will have no copy of chromosome 21.

Potential consequences of fertilization:

*   **Fertilization by a gamete with an extra chromosome 21:** If this gamete fertilizes a normal gamete (with one copy of chromosome 21), the resulting zygote will have three copies of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21). This causes Down syndrome.
*   **Fertilization by a gamete missing chromosome 21:** If this gamete fertilizes a normal gamete, the resulting zygote will have only one copy of chromosome 21 (monosomy 21). This is usually lethal, and the embryo will not survive.

**1. Question 1: (10 marks)
Discuss the significance of cell division in living organisms. Elaborate on the different phases of the cell cycle, with a particular focus on the events occurring during Interphase (G1, S, and G2 phases) and M phase (Mitosis). Explain how these phases contribute to the accurate duplication and distribution of genetic material. What are the consequences of errors occurring during the cell cycle, and how are these errors typically addressed within the cell? Use diagrams to illustrate your answer.**

**Answer:**

**Significance of Cell Division:**

Cell division is a fundamental process in all living organisms. Its significance stems from its roles in:

*   **Growth:** In multicellular organisms, cell division allows for the increase in cell number, leading to growth and development.
*   **Repair:** Damaged tissues and organs can be repaired through cell division, replacing old or injured cells with new ones.
*   **Reproduction:** In unicellular organisms, cell division is the primary mode of reproduction (asexual reproduction). In multicellular organisms, meiosis (a specialized form of cell division) produces gametes for sexual reproduction.
*   **Maintenance:** Cell division maintains tissue homeostasis by replacing cells that have reached the end of their lifespan or have been lost due to injury.

**Phases of the Cell Cycle:**

The cell cycle is a highly regulated sequence of events that leads to cell division. It is divided into two main phases: Interphase and M phase (Mitosis).

**I. Interphase:**

Interphase is the longest phase of the cell cycle, during which the cell grows, duplicates its DNA, and prepares for division. It consists of three sub-phases:

*   **G1 Phase (Gap 1):**
    *   **Events:** Cell growth and metabolism, synthesis of proteins and organelles, monitoring of external signals for cell division.
    *   **Contribution to Accurate Duplication:** Ensures the cell has enough resources (proteins, organelles, energy) for DNA replication and cell division.
*   **S Phase (Synthesis):**
    *   **Events:** DNA replication, resulting in the duplication of each chromosome to form two identical sister chromatids.
    *   **Contribution to Accurate Duplication:** The faithful replication of DNA is crucial to ensure that each daughter cell receives a complete and identical copy of the genetic material.
*   **G2 Phase (Gap 2):**
    *   **Events:** Continued cell growth, synthesis of proteins required for mitosis, duplication of organelles, checking for DNA damage or errors in replication.
    *   **Contribution to Accurate Duplication:** Provides a final check on DNA integrity and ensures the cell is fully prepared for mitosis.

**II. M Phase (Mitosis):**

Mitosis is the process of nuclear division, followed by cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division), resulting in two genetically identical daughter cells. Mitosis has four main stages:

*   **Prophase:** Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, the nuclear envelope breaks down, and the mitotic spindle begins to form.
    *   **Contribution to Accurate Distribution:** Chromosome condensation facilitates the proper segregation of chromosomes during subsequent stages.
*   **Metaphase:** Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate (equator of the cell). The spindle fibers attach to the centromeres of each chromosome.
    *   **Contribution to Accurate Distribution:** Ensures that each chromosome is correctly attached to the spindle fibers and is ready for segregation.
*   **Anaphase:** Sister chromatids separate and move towards opposite poles of the cell, pulled by the spindle fibers.
    *   **Contribution to Accurate Distribution:** Equal segregation of sister chromatids ensures that each daughter cell receives a complete and identical set of chromosomes.
*   **Telophase:** Chromosomes arrive at the poles of the cell and begin to decondense. The nuclear envelope reforms around each set of chromosomes, forming two separate nuclei.
    *   **Contribution to Accurate Distribution:** Completion of nuclear division, setting the stage for cytokinesis.

**Consequences of Errors in the Cell Cycle:**

Errors during the cell cycle can have severe consequences:

*   **Mutations:** Errors in DNA replication can lead to mutations, which can disrupt cell function, cause uncontrolled cell growth (cancer), or lead to cell death.
*   **Aneuploidy:** Errors in chromosome segregation (non-disjunction) can result in daughter cells with an abnormal number of chromosomes (aneuploidy). This can lead to developmental abnormalities or cell death.
*   **Uncontrolled Cell Growth:** Defects in cell cycle regulation can lead to uncontrolled cell growth and the formation of tumors.

**Cellular Mechanisms to Address Errors:**

Cells have several mechanisms to prevent or correct errors during the cell cycle:

*   **DNA Repair Mechanisms:** Enzymes that can detect and repair damaged or incorrectly copied DNA.
*   **Cell Cycle Checkpoints:** Surveillance mechanisms that monitor the integrity of DNA and the proper progression of the cell cycle. If errors are detected, the cell cycle is arrested, allowing time for repair. The main checkpoints include:
    *   **G1 Checkpoint:** Checks for DNA damage, cell size, and nutrient availability.
    *   **G2 Checkpoint:** Checks for DNA replication completeness and DNA damage.
    *   **M Checkpoint (Spindle Checkpoint):** Checks for proper chromosome alignment and attachment to spindle fibers.
*   **Apoptosis:** If the cell cannot repair the damage, it may undergo programmed cell death (apoptosis), preventing the propagation of errors to daughter cells.

**2. Question 2: (10 marks)
Compare and contrast mitosis and meiosis, highlighting their roles in different types of organisms and their significance in heredity. Your discussion should include:
*   The purpose of each type of cell division.
*   The number of cell divisions and the resulting number of daughter cells.
*   The chromosome number in the daughter cells compared to the parent cell (haploid vs. diploid).

*   The specific stages within each process (e.g., Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase) and the key events during each stage, using diagrams where appropriate.**

**Answer:**

Mitosis and meiosis are two distinct types of cell division with different purposes and outcomes.

**Comparison of Mitosis and Meiosis:**

| Feature                | Mitosis                                                         | Meiosis                                                              |
| ---------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Purpose**            | Growth, repair, asexual reproduction                          | Sexual reproduction, gamete formation                               |
| **Organisms**          | All eukaryotes (unicellular and multicellular)                    | Eukaryotes that reproduce sexually                                    |
| **Cell Type**          | Somatic (body) cells                                            | Germ cells (reproductive organs)                                       |
| **# of Divisions**    | 1                                                               | 2 (Meiosis I and Meiosis II)                                          |
| **# of Daughter Cells** | 2                                                               | 4                                                                    |

| **Crossing Over**      | Absent                                                          | Present during Prophase I                                            |
| **Significance in Heredity** | Maintains genetic continuity during cell division and tissue growth | Introduces genetic variation and ensures the correct chromosome number in offspring |

**Mitosis:**

*   **Purpose:** Mitosis is essential for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction in eukaryotes. It ensures that each daughter cell receives a complete and identical copy of the genetic material.
*   **Number of Divisions:** Mitosis involves a single cell division.
*   **Chromosome Number:** The daughter cells have the same chromosome number as the parent cell (diploid).
*   **Crossing Over:** Crossing over does not occur during mitosis.
*   **Stages:**
    *   **Prophase:** Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes, nuclear envelope breaks down, and the mitotic spindle forms.
    *   **Metaphase:** Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate.
    *   **Anaphase:** Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.
    *   **Telophase:** Chromosomes arrive at the poles, nuclear envelope reforms, and chromosomes decondense.
*   **Significance in Heredity:** Mitosis maintains genetic continuity by producing genetically identical daughter cells. This ensures that all cells in an organism have the same genetic information, enabling coordinated development and function.

**Meiosis:**

*   **Purpose:** Meiosis is a specialized form of cell division that occurs in sexually reproducing organisms. Its purpose is to produce haploid gametes (sperm and egg) with half the chromosome number of the parent cell.
*   **Number of Divisions:** Meiosis involves two sequential divisions: Meiosis I and Meiosis II.
*   **Chromosome Number:** The daughter cells (gametes) have half the chromosome number of the parent cell (haploid).
*   **Crossing Over:** Crossing over occurs during Prophase I of meiosis, where homologous chromosomes exchange genetic material.
*   **Stages:**
    *   **Meiosis I:**
        *   **Prophase I:** Chromosomes condense, homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis), crossing over occurs.
        *   **Metaphase I:** Homologous chromosome pairs align at the metaphase plate.
        *   **Anaphase I:** Homologous chromosomes separate and move to opposite poles.
        *   **Telophase I:** Chromosomes arrive at the poles, and the cell divides.
    *   **Meiosis II:**
        *   **Prophase II:** Chromosomes condense again.
        *   **Metaphase II:** Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate.
        *   **Anaphase II:** Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.
        *   **Telophase II:** Chromosomes arrive at the poles, and the cell divides, resulting in four haploid daughter cells.
*   **Significance in Heredity:**
    *   **Chromosome Number Reduction:** Meiosis ensures that the chromosome number is halved during gamete formation. This is essential for maintaining the correct chromosome number in sexually reproducing organisms because the fusion of two haploid gametes during fertilization restores the diploid chromosome number in the offspring.
    *   **Genetic Variation:** Meiosis generates genetic variation through crossing over and independent assortment of chromosomes. This genetic variation is essential for adaptation and evolution, as it allows populations to respond to changing environments.

**Answer:**

Here's a description of chromosome behavior and DNA amount during meiosis for a cell with 2n = 36:

*   **G1 Phase of Interphase:**
    *   **Chromosome Behavior:** Chromosomes are in an uncondensed state (chromatin). There are 36 chromosomes, each consisting of a single DNA molecule.

*   **Metaphase I of Meiosis:**
    *   **Chromosome Behavior:** Tetrads (homologous chromosome pairs) align at the metaphase plate. Each chromosome is still duplicated, consisting of two sister chromatids.
    *   **DNA Amount:** 4C.

    *   **Chromosome Behavior:** Homologous chromosomes separate and move to opposite poles of the cell. Sister chromatids remain attached at the centromere. Each pole receives 18 duplicated chromosomes (each consisting of two sister chromatids).

    *   **Chromosome Behavior:** Chromosomes condense again. There are 18 duplicated chromosomes in each cell.
    *   **DNA Amount:** 2C.

    *   **Chromosome Behavior:** Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate.
    *   **DNA Amount:** 2C.

    *   **Chromosome Behavior:** Sister chromatids separate during Anaphase II. Cytokinesis occurs, resulting in four haploid daughter cells. Each cell contains 18 chromosomes, each consisting of a single DNA molecule.

**Significance of Chromosome Segregation:**

Chromosome segregation during meiosis is crucial for maintaining the chromosome number across generations in sexually reproducing organisms. Meiosis reduces the chromosome number from diploid (2n) to haploid (n) in the gametes (sperm and egg). When a sperm fertilizes an egg, the resulting zygote has the diploid number of chromosomes (2n), with one set of chromosomes from each parent. This ensures that the chromosome number remains constant from generation to generation.

**Errors in Meiosis and Aneuploidy:**

Errors in chromosome segregation during meiosis can lead to aneuploidy, which is a condition in which cells have an abnormal number of chromosomes. The most common cause of aneuploidy is non-disjunction, the failure of chromosomes or sister chromatids to separate properly during meiosis.

    *   **Cancer:** Aneuploidy has also been linked to an increased risk of cancer.

In summary, meiosis ensures the correct chromosome number in offspring and generates genetic variation. Errors in meiosis can have serious consequences, leading to aneuploidy and developmental abnormalities.

This chapter explores the fascinating structural diversity of flowering plants (angiosperms). It covers the morphology (external form) of key plant parts: roots, stems, leaves, flowers, fruits, and seeds. It also introduces important concepts like phyllotaxy (leaf arrangement), inflorescence (flower arrangement), aestivation (arrangement of petals/sepals), and placentation (ovule arrangement within the ovary). Finally, it touches upon how to describe a flowering plant in a semi-technical way using floral diagrams and formulas and gives the example of the Solanaceae family.

**Definitions in Simple Terms:**

*   **Morphology:** The study of the external form and structure of organisms, in this case, focusing on plants. It's like plant anatomy but looking at the *outside*.
*   **Root System (Taproot vs. Fibrous):** The underground part of the plant. A taproot system has one main, thick root (like a carrot), while a fibrous root system has many thin roots (like grass).
*   **Shoot System:** The above-ground part of the plant, consisting of the stem, leaves, and flowers.
*   **Node:** The point on a stem where a leaf or branch originates.
*   **Internode:** The section of stem between two nodes.
*   **Leaf Venation (Reticulate vs. Parallel):** The pattern of veins in a leaf. Reticulate venation looks like a network (typical of dicots), while parallel venation has veins running parallel to each other (typical of monocots).
*   **Inflorescence (Racemose vs. Cymose):** The arrangement of flowers on a plant. Racemose inflorescences have a main axis that continues to grow, with flowers blooming from the bottom up. Cymose inflorescences have a main axis that *ends* in a flower, with flowers blooming from the top down.
*   **Actinomorphic vs. Zygomorphic Flower:** Terms describing floral symmetry. Actinomorphic flowers have radial symmetry (like a star), meaning they can be divided into equal halves along multiple planes. Zygomorphic flowers have bilateral symmetry, meaning they can only be divided into equal halves along one plane.
*   **Aestivation:** The arrangement of sepals or petals in a flower bud relative to one another.
*   **Placentation:** The arrangement of ovules (which become seeds) inside the ovary of a flower.

**2. Definitions (Key Concepts):**

*   **Morphology:** This is the study of the external form and structure of plants. It involves describing the different parts of a plant (root, stem, leaves, flowers, fruits, seeds) and their characteristics.
*   **Anatomy:** This refers to the internal structure of plants, often studied through dissection and microscopy. It looks at the arrangement of tissues and cells within the plant organs.
*   **Root System:** The part of the plant that is typically underground, primarily responsible for absorbing water and nutrients from the soil, and anchoring the plant. It can be a taproot system (with a main root) or a fibrous root system (many similar roots).
*   **Shoot System:** The above-ground part of the plant, consisting of the stem, leaves, flowers, and fruits. It's responsible for support, photosynthesis, reproduction, and dispersal.
*   **Inflorescence:** The arrangement of flowers on the floral axis (the stalk or branch that bears the flowers). There are two main types: racemose (flowers arranged along the axis in an acropetal manner) and cymose (main axis ends in a flower).
*   **Aestivation:** The arrangement of sepals or petals in a flower bud relative to each other. It can be valvate (touching), twisted (overlapping in a spiral), imbricate (overlapping without a pattern), or vexillary (a special type found in pea flowers).
*   **Placentation:** The arrangement of ovules (which become seeds) within the ovary of a flower. Different types include marginal, axile, parietal, free central, and basal.

The floral formula is: Ebr ⊕ K(5) C(5) A5 G(2)

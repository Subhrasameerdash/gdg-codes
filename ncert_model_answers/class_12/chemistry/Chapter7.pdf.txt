### Definitions ###
Okay, here's a breakdown of the NCERT Chapter on Alcohols, Phenols, and Ethers, explained in my own words:

**Summary:**

This chapter introduces three important classes of organic compounds: alcohols, phenols, and ethers. It covers their classification, nomenclature (IUPAC naming), methods of preparation, physical properties (like boiling points and solubility), and chemical reactions. The chapter emphasizes the relationship between structure and properties, and how the functional groups (-OH in alcohols/phenols, -O- in ethers) dictate their reactivity.

**Definitions:**

*   **Alcohols:** These are organic compounds where a hydroxyl (-OH) group is directly attached to a saturated carbon atom (an sp3 hybridized carbon) in an alkyl group. Think of it as replacing a hydrogen in an alkane with -OH.
*   **Phenols:** Similar to alcohols, but here the -OH group is directly attached to a carbon atom that is part of an aromatic ring (like benzene).
*   **Ethers:** These are compounds containing an oxygen atom connected to two alkyl or aryl groups (R-O-R' or Ar-O-Ar'). You can think of them as derivatives of water (H-O-H) where both hydrogen atoms have been replaced by organic groups.
*   **IUPAC Nomenclature:** A standardized system for naming organic compounds. For alcohols, the parent alkane name is modified to end in "-ol," with numbers indicating the position of the -OH group. For ethers, one alkyl group and oxygen is treated as an alkoxy substituent on the other alkyl group.
*   **Acidity:** Alcohols and phenols can act as weak acids, donating a proton (H+). Phenols are generally more acidic than alcohols due to the stabilization of the phenoxide ion through resonance.
*   **Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution:** Reactions where an electrophile (electron-seeking species) replaces a hydrogen atom on an aromatic ring. The -OH group in phenols makes the benzene ring more reactive and directs substitution to the ortho and para positions.
*   **Williamson Ether Synthesis:** A method for preparing ethers by reacting an alkyl halide with an alkoxide (R-O-).
